History

In 2011, at the age of 16, Dutch innovator Boyan Slat encountered an alarming abundance of plastic debris compared to marine life during a diving expedition in Greece. This eye-opening experience spurred him to channel his efforts into a high school project aimed at delving deeper into the issue of ocean plastic pollution and understanding the prevailing belief that cleaning it up was deemed impossible. Since then, Boyan Slat has evolved into a prominent environmental entrepreneur and the founder of The Ocean Cleanup, a nonprofit organization dedicated to developing advanced technologies to remove plastic waste from the world's oceans. The project has garnered global attention and support, showcasing Slat's commitment to addressing one of the most pressing environmental challenges of our time. His innovative solutions and ambitious initiatives continue to contribute to the ongoing fight against ocean plastic pollution.

Boyan Slat made the decision to discontinue his aerospace engineering studies at TU Delft to fully commit his time to developing his groundbreaking idea. In 2013, he went on to establish The Ocean Cleanup with the overarching goal of creating advanced technologies to effectively eliminate plastic pollution from the world's oceans. The organization's mission revolves around the development and implementation of innovative solutions to address this pressing environmental issue. In a significant milestone for The Ocean Cleanup, a comprehensive 528-page feasibility study detailing the potential of the project was published in June 2014. This study likely delved into the technical aspects, environmental impact, and logistical considerations of the proposed initiatives. The publication of such a study underscores the organization's commitment to transparency and the dissemination of valuable information related to their ambitious undertaking. Ocean Cleanup

The filtering system uses passive floating structures localized in the ocean gyres. These structures act as a containment boom. The floater drifts with the wind, waves and ocean currents to capture marine debris. A permeable screen underneath the float catches subsurface debris. The system is unmanned and incorporates solar-powered monitoring and navigation systems, including GPS, cameras, lanterns and an automatic identification system (AIS) for vessel traffic services.

System 001, deployed in September 2018, consisted of a 800 meters long barrier with a 3 meters wide skirt that hangs beneath it. System 001 encountered difficulties retaining the plastic collected. After two month of operation, during which the system had collected 2,000 kilograms of plastic, mechanical stress caused an 18-meter section of the structure to detach. After several months of root cause analysis and redesign, the organization employed System 001/B which could successfully capture and collect plastic, and even microplastics.

At the end of July 2021, the organization revisited the Great Pacific Garbage Patch to trial System 002-- a revamped version of the previous systems with an increased span of 1,600 metres. The new technology claims to capture 1,000 kg of plastics per day. As of 25 July 2022, The Ocean Cleanup announced to have removed a total of 100,000 kg of plastic from the Great Pacific Garbage Patch.

The organization is now developing System 003, which is planned to have a three times bigger plastic intake than the current design. Unlike the previous prototypes, this new system is meant to be a blueprint for an entire fleet of filtering systems able to meet the scale of the problem.

River Interception

Addressing concerns about the maintenance and cost-effectiveness of filtering devices closer to shore, the organization has continuously evolved its approach. In a significant update in October 2019, the organization introduced the River Interceptor, a cutting-edge technology designed to enhance plastic waste recovery in water bodies. Powered by solar energy, the Interceptor vessels employ innovative floating arms to efficiently guide floating waste towards a collection conveyor. This conveyor seamlessly deposits the gathered trash into dumpsters situated on a detachable barge positioned below the Interceptor. The use of this advanced technology aims to optimize the collection process, ensuring a more effective and economical means of plastic recovery.

As of October 2022, The Ocean Cleanup enterntains eight Interceptors in Indonesia, Malaysia, Viet Nam, Dominican Republic, USA and Jamaica.
